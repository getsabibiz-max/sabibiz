Why You Can Be Selling Every Day and Still Broke

4 min read · Money & Profit Basics

You opened the shop early. Customers came. Money changed hands all day. By evening your hand has touched more cash than most people see in a week. And yet — by the end of the month, there's nothing left. No savings, no growth, sometimes not even enough to restock.

If this sounds familiar, the problem usually isn't how hard you're working. It's that you're tracking cash, not profit — and those are two very different things.

Cash in hand is not the same as profit

Cash flow is money moving in and out of your hands today. Profit is what's actually left over after every cost is paid — stock, transport, fees, the small "settling" payments, even the data you bought to confirm an order. Most of that money was never yours to keep; it was just passing through your hands on its way to someone else.

A business can look busy and still be losing money, quietly, every single day — because the owner is reading their cash, not their costs.

Three silent profit killers

  • Untracked small expenses. Transport, "ageru," wrapper, recharge card — each one feels too small to write down. Added up over a month, they often equal a week's profit.
  • Mixed money. When business and personal money sit in the same pocket or account, you can't see your real numbers — and you'll always spend more than the business can afford.
  • Selling below true cost. If your price only covers what you paid the supplier — and not transport, fees, and waste — every sale quietly loses money, no matter how many you make.
Quick example: You buy a wig for ₦7,000 and sell it for ₦8,000. Looks like ₦1,000 profit. But add ₦400 transport and ₦300 market fees, and your real cost is ₦7,700 — leaving just ₦300. Sell ten like that in a week and you've made ₦3,000, not the ₦10,000 it felt like.

How to know your real number

The fix isn't complicated, but it does require honesty: write down every cost — not just the big ones — and compare it against every sale. Do that for two weeks and you'll know, for the first time, whether your business is actually profitable or just busy.
